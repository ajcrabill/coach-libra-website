Launching your book

Your book is written.
Now let's get it to the
people who need it.

A launch has one real job: reach the readers your book is for. They're the ones who get excited, talk about it, review it, and pass it on. Word of mouth from real readers is the best marketing there is, and this page shows you how to earn it, stage by stage.

See the plan → Three stages, three checklists.

One goal, everything else is detail.

Marketing hacks and paid ads have their place, but they're not the point. The most successful launches do one thing well: they reach the readers the book is most for, and let those readers do the rest. Get enough of them talking in the first week and the math flips. Before launch, you serve the book. After a strong launch, the book starts serving you, bringing the right people to you for years.

That first week matters most, especially on Amazon, where early sales and reviews shape how the store recommends your book to everyone who comes after. So the plan below front-loads the work: most of it happens before launch day, so the day itself is mostly hitting send.

The three groups of people you're reaching.

Ⅰ

Friends & family

Thirty to fifty people you can count on for a review in launch week. You have more than you think: old classmates, colleagues, clients you've helped, the people who reply to you most online, your spouse's and your kids' networks. Look through your phone and LinkedIn. They're there.

Ⅱ

Your core team

The handful who'll go further: review it, post about it, recommend it to their own people, and help you spread the word. Usually close friends and clients who love your work. Treat them well, bring them in early, and they'll carry you.

Ⅲ

Wider readers

The people who'd buy and benefit but don't know you yet. Think about what they read and listen to, and who they already trust. Reaching them is the bonus; your core team is the foundation.

The plan, in three stages.

Give yourself room. Six weeks is the bare minimum; a couple of months is far more comfortable. Read the whole thing once before you start, since some of the timing depends on what you decide to do.

Before launch

  1. Set your launch price to $0.99. This is your launch price, not your list price, and it's a deliberate move. A low price lifts sales two ways: it lowers the bar to buy, and it lets you into the promotional newsletters below. A burst of sales in week one can lift your book's rank in its Amazon categories, and Amazon rewards a strong start by recommending your book to new readers on its own. (If you're applying for a BookBub deal, keep the book at full price until they approve, then drop it on their schedule.)
  2. Line up paid launch-day promotions. Several newsletters email book deals to big lists of readers. Set them to start on launch day and run through the first week. They have lead times, so apply early. BookBub is the strongest and books out furthest ahead. Amazon's sponsored ads also convert well; you may not profit at $0.99, but the right keywords can drive real sales, and you can keep the winners running long after.
  3. Write your friends & family. Send them an advance copy about a month out and tell them you'll ask for an Amazon review on launch day. Their job is simple: post that review. (A heads-up: never pay for reviews, it's against Amazon's rules. Sending an advance copy is not payment, so send as many as you like. Almost anyone with an active account can review, even without buying.)
  4. Organize your core team. Give them an advance copy too, and a little more: invite them into a private group, share behind-the-scenes notes, make them feel like insiders. On launch day you'll ask them to review and to do a bit more, like a LinkedIn post, a note to people who'd benefit, a share. Make every ask specific.
  5. Build your high-value relationships. Influencers, people who run relevant groups, podcast hosts whose listeners are your readers, anyone with reach into your audience. Send them an advance copy at least a month out (if you want a blurb, ask as early as you can). When you ask for help, be specific: a post on launch day, a podcast interview, a review on their blog. Write the follow-up emails now so they're ready to send.
  6. Reach out to media. Where does your audience get its information? A local radio segment, a niche blog, an association newsletter. Tell the people who run it exactly why their audience will care about your book. Slots fill weeks ahead, so ask early, and write your follow-ups in advance.
  7. Grow your wider audience. Optional, and entirely up to you: a lead magnet to build an email list, LinkedIn connections, shareable posts, a short series. Do all of it, some of it, or none of it. Your core team is what matters most; this is extra.
  8. Consider a Goodreads giveaway. The most optional item here. Giving away copies can build a little awareness and a few reviews, but only a small share of winners review, so the returns drop off fast. If you do it, end it a week or two before launch so readers have time, and remember Amazon only allows reviews on or after launch day.

Launch day

If you did the work above, today is mostly sending the emails you already wrote.

  1. Email your friends & family. The book is live. Ask plainly for that Amazon review (they can't post before today, so the launch-day ask is the one that counts).
  2. Rally your core team. Ask them to post their reviews and do the extras you lined up. If you've been talking with them in a group, be especially present today.
  3. Follow up with your high-value relationships. Blurbs should be in by now; nudge anyone who promised a launch-day post, and confirm scheduled interviews. And say thank you. Gratitude goes a long way.
  4. Follow up with media. Same idea, same thanks. Reporters keep the people who are gracious and available in mind for the next story.
  5. Send your newsletter. If you have an email list, today's the day. If you don't, that's fine, you don't need one for a strong launch.
  6. Post where you're active. Optional, but if you have a following anywhere, including LinkedIn, let them know the book is out.

Launch week

  1. Nudge friends & family who haven't reviewed. A gentle reminder mid-week. Every review in this first week helps.
  2. Send a follow-up with early reviews. Gather the best lines from readers, media, and Amazon, and send your list a second note a few days in featuring them.
  3. Share the rave reviews. Post them on LinkedIn and anywhere else you're active. It can feel like bragging; it isn't. It's how the next reader who needs your book finds it.

After the week, the book works for you.

Launch week was about serving the book. Once it's done, that effort starts paying you back. A book that landed well keeps bringing people to you, the readers, the clients, the conversations, long after you've stopped pushing. That's the real return on a careful launch, and it's why it's worth it.
